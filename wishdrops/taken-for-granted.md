There are some things that are taken for granted in wishdropland that we forget most people probably don't think about and also probably don't care. One is the degree to which the human body has adapted to the laws of physics. What we're really trying to say is that we didn't end up this way semi-arbitrarily and oh wow turns out we can see these colors and hear these notes and feel these feelings as we explore the universe. We're thinking it's the other way around. The universe constantly whispers secret harmonies for every kind of sense and the human body not to mention all the other animals tune into them and evolve in accordance.

What the heck does that even mean ?

It might come as a surprise, for example, that we the wishdrops maintain a precise ratio between the wavelength of light emitted when an electron falls into the ground state of an atom and the wavelength at the root of the color spectrum. Heck, maybe it's a surprise to hear that wavelengths have anything to do with colors at all, so we should probably start there.

Even though sense of color kinda seems like a subjective experience, it isn't. Not fully, anyway. Sure, a color like red might look different to different people in a way we'll never know, but generally we always agree when something is red. And that's not really a surprise because there's a physical signature to seeing red (not the mad kind). The light that enters our eyeballs has a wavelength that we can measure, and when that light is peak red, something we can agree upon more or less, quirkiness and colorblindness notwithstanding, it has a wavelength of just about 670 nanometers. Yellow ? Right around 580 nanometers. Indeed, the color spectrum ranges from infrared somewhere near 750 nanometers down to ultraviolet which kicks in around 375 nanometers. Maybe you've seen images of the color spectrum that graduates through the colors over a number line that counts in nanometers, and indeed, this is a pretty compelling exhibit ! What we're saying is that if we visually scan laterally across such a display we should be able to identify the peaks -- pure colors -- and take note of their wavelengths. Our proposal is that they abide by this formula:

* 160 / 4pi / R(inf) / sqrt(n) for n in integers 1 ... 10:
  * n = 1 color spectrum root 1160nm
  * n = 2 ir onset
  * n = 3 red 670nm
  * n = 4 yellow 580nm
  * n = 5 green 518nm
  * n = 6 blue 474nm
  * n = 7 indigo 438nm
  * n = 8 violet 410nm
  * n = 9 uv onset 387nm
  * n = 10 color spectrum bookend 366nm
