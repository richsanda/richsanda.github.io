>Capitalism is defined by capital — wealth deployed to generate more wealth — and its core structural requirement is endless growth. Not growth as a side effect, but as a necessity baked into how debt, investment, and returns work. Which is why "conscious capitalism" or "green growth" tend to be contradictions in terms — you can soften the edges, but you can't opt out of the growth imperative and still be operating within the system.
>
>In 1972, a team of MIT scientists led by Donella Meadows modeled the entire global system — population, resources, pollution, food, industrial output — all interacting. Their conclusion was simple and explosive: you cannot have infinite exponential growth on a finite planet. In a business-as-usual scenario, their models showed industrial civilization overshooting planetary limits and collapsing around mid-century. Not human extinction — but the breakdown of the complex systems we all depend on. Follow-up analyses as recently as 2023 show real-world data tracking that original collapse scenario pretty closely.
>
>The interesting part is that serious alternatives exist and are well-developed. Economists like Herman Daly proposed steady-state economies that improve quality of life without expanding physical resource consumption. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics — now actual city policy in Amsterdam — sets a social floor (everyone's basic needs met) and an ecological ceiling (planetary limits respected), with the goal being to live between them. Degrowth theorists argue wealthy nations need to deliberately shrink material consumption before it's forced on them chaotically. Nobel laureate Elinor Ostrom demonstrated that communities can manage shared resources sustainably without markets or top-down control — dismantling the assumption that privatization is the only alternative to overexploitation.
>
>These frameworks converge on a few shared ideas: replace GDP with metrics that actually measure wellbeing; democratize ownership through cooperatives and public stewardship; decouple survival from endless labor; and rebuild more local, resilient economies.
>
>The intellectual foundation is genuinely there. What's missing isn't ideas or even evidence — it's the political will, which tends to be captured by those who benefit most from the current system. As Meadows herself wrote, **the deepest leverage point for changing any system is its paradigm — the shared, unstated assumptions about how the world works** [my emphasis]. The assumptions that growth is good, that nature is a resource to be converted, that there are no limits. Those assumptions are what actually need to change.

> 
>On The Limits to Growth itself — it's still worth reading, but it's dense and dated in places. The 30-year update (published 2004 as Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update) is actually more useful because it incorporates decades of real-world data validating the original models. If you're going to read one version, read that one.
Five books to warm up to these ideas, roughly in order of accessibility:
>1. Less is More — Jason Hickel — probably the best entry point right now. Readable, passionate, covers the history of how we got here and what an alternative could look like. It scrutinizes perpetual economic growth while identifying seven critical ways mainstream economics has led us astray, and sets out a roadmap for meeting the needs of all within the means of the planet.
